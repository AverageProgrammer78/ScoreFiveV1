Topic 1.1: Moles and Molar Mass

Mole

A mole (mol) is the amount of substance that contains 6.022 × 10²³ particles. This number is also called Avogadro's number.

NA = 6.022 × 10²³ particles/mol

Particles can be: atoms, molecules, ions or formula units.

FREE TIP: Always label what kind of particle you're counting!

Why We Can't Count Particles Directly

Atoms are microscopic and lab balances measure grams, not atoms. We need a bridge between mass and number of particles and that bridge is mole + molar mass.

Molar Mass (THE MOST IMPORTANT SKILL)

Molar mass = mass of 1 mole of a substance. Units = grams per mole (g/mol). You can find the Molar Mass by using the periodic table.

Example: H₂O

  • 1 mole of H₂O = 18.02 g
  • 1 mole of H₂O = 6.022 × 10²³ molecules

The AMU Connection

Atomic mass unit is used for single atoms or molecules. Too small for lab measurements.

Key Rule: The mass of 1 particle in amu is numerically equal to the molar mass in grams.

Example: One oxygen atom → 16 amu | One mole of oxygen atoms = 16 g

CORE EQUATION YOU MUST KNOW

n = m/M

Where: n = moles, m = mass (grams), M = molar mass (g/mol)

Conversions You MUST Master

Grams → Moles:

Grams × (1 mole / molar mass)

Moles → Particles:

moles × 6.022 × 10²³

Grams → Particles: Two steps: (1) grams → moles, (2) moles → particles

Topic 1.2: Mass Spectra of Elements

Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element that have the same number of protons, different numbers of neutrons, and different masses. Same element → same chemical behavior. Different mass → affects atomic mass.

Mass Spectrum

A mass spectrum shows the masses of isotopes and the relative abundance of each isotope.

  • x-axis → mass (amu)
  • y-axis → relative abundance
  • Each peak represents an isotope
  • Height of peak = how common that isotope is

Average Atomic Mass

The average atomic mass is the weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes. This value appears on the periodic table.

Weighted Average Formula:
Average Atomic Mass = Σ (isotope mass × fractional abundance)

Topic 1.3: Elemental Composition of Pure Substances

Molecular vs Ionic Substances

Molecular: Consist of discrete molecules held by covalent bonds (H₂O, CO₂)

Ionic: Consist of ions in a crystal lattice, described using formula units (NaCl, CaF₂)

Law of Definite Proportions

In any pure sample of a compound, the ratio of the masses of the constituent elements is always the same.

Empirical Formula

The chemical formula showing the lowest whole number ratio of atoms.

Topic 1.5: Atomic Structure and Electron Configuration

Structure of the Atom

  • Protons: positive, in nucleus
  • Neutrons: no charge, in nucleus
  • Electrons: negative, outside nucleus

Coulomb's Law

Fcoulombic ∝ (q₁q₂)/r²

Attraction increases with greater nuclear charge and decreases with greater distance.

Subshells and Capacities

Aufbau Principle: Electrons fill lowest energy levels first.

Core vs Valence Electrons

  • Core: Inner electrons, don't participate in bonding
  • Valence: Outermost electrons, determine reactivity

Ionization Energy

Energy required to remove an electron. Depends on distance from nucleus, shielding, and effective nuclear charge.

Topic 1.6: Photoelectron Spectroscopy (PES)

PES measures binding energy of electrons - how much energy is required to remove them.

PES Spectrum

  • x-axis → binding energy
  • y-axis → relative number of electrons
  • Each peak = a specific subshell
  • Higher binding energy = closer to nucleus, stronger attraction
  • Peak height = number of electrons in that subshell

PES experimentally confirms electron configuration and the Aufbau principle.

Topic 1.7: Periodic Trends

The periodic table is organized by repeating patterns in atomic properties.

Key Trends

Ionization Energy: Increases across period, decreases down group

Atomic Radius: Decreases across period, increases down group

Ionic Radius: Cations smaller than neutral atoms, anions larger

Electron Affinity: More negative across period, less negative down group

Electronegativity: Increases across period, decreases down group (Fluorine is most electronegative)

Topic 1.8: Valence Electrons and Ionic Compounds

Reactivity Trends

Metals: Reactivity increases down a group (easier to lose electrons)

Nonmetals: Reactivity increases up a group (stronger attraction for electrons)
