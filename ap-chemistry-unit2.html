Topic 2.1: Types of Chemical Bonds

Electronegativity Trends

Electronegativity describes how strongly an atom attracts electrons when bonded to another atom.

Periodic Trends

  • Across a period (left → right): electronegativity increases
  • Down a group (top → bottom): electronegativity decreases

Why These Trends Occur

a) Electronic Structure & Shell Model

Atoms have energy levels (shells). As you go down a group, atoms have:

  • More shells
  • Greater distance between nucleus and valence electrons
  • Weaker attraction for bonding electrons

As you go across a period:

  • Number of shells stays the same
  • Nuclear charge increases
  • Stronger pull on electrons

b) Coulomb's Law (Qualitative Understanding)

Coulomb's Law explains attraction between charged particles:

  • Greater nuclear charge → stronger attraction
  • Greater distance → weaker attraction
Key Insight:
• Across a period: stronger attraction → higher EN
• Down a group: weaker attraction → lower EN
You do NOT need the equation for Coulomb's Law here — just the concept.

Nonpolar Covalent Bonds

A nonpolar covalent bond forms when valence electrons are shared equally between atoms.

When Do Nonpolar Bonds Form?

  • Atoms have equal or very similar electronegativities
  • ΔEN (difference in electronegativity) ≈ 0
Example: Carbon–Hydrogen (C–H)
Carbon is slightly more electronegative than hydrogen, but the difference is small enough that electrons are shared nearly equally. This makes C–H bonds effectively nonpolar.

Polar Covalent Bonds

A polar covalent bond forms when atoms with unequal electronegativities share electrons unequally.

i. Partial Charges

  • The atom with higher electronegativity pulls electrons closer
  • That atom becomes partially negative (δ⁻)
  • The other atom becomes partially positive (δ⁺)

ii. Bond Dipoles in Single Bonds

The arrow shows direction of polarity:

  • Arrow points toward the more electronegative atom
  • The tail (with a cross) is at the atom with partial positive charge (δ⁺)
  • Larger electronegativity differences → larger bond dipole

iii. Ionic Character & Bonding Continuum

Bonding exists on a continuum:

Nonpolar Covalent → Polar Covalent → Ionic

All polar covalent bonds have some ionic character, and ionic bonds represent the end of the spectrum.

Summary Table

Bond Type Electronegativity Difference Electron Sharing Charges Present
Nonpolar Covalent Very small / zero Equal None
Polar Covalent Moderate Unequal Partial (δ⁺ / δ⁻)
Ionic Large Transferred Full charges

Bond Type Is Not Determined by Electronegativity Alone

While electronegativity difference (ΔEN) is important, it is not the only factor used to classify a bond as ionic or covalent.

General Classification Rule

  • Metal + nonmetal → ionic bonding
  • Nonmetal + nonmetal → covalent bonding

This rule works because:

  • Metals tend to lose electrons easily
  • Nonmetals tend to gain or attract electrons
  • This leads to electron transfer, which is characteristic of ionic bonding
This is a general guideline, not an absolute rule.

Why ΔEN Alone Is Insufficient

  • Some bonds have moderate ΔEN but behave more like ionic compounds
  • Other bonds may have a large ΔEN but still show covalent characteristics
  • Bonding exists on a spectrum, not in rigid categories

Properties Must Be Considered

The best way to determine whether a compound is ionic or covalent is by looking at its properties, such as:

  • Melting and boiling points
  • Electrical conductivity
  • Solubility
  • Brittleness or malleability

Properties reflect the actual bonding and structure, not just a rule of thumb.

Metallic Solids & Delocalized Electrons

In a metallic solid, the valence electrons are not associated with any one atom — they are delocalized and free to move throughout the structure.

What "Delocalized Electrons" Means

  • Electrons do not stay fixed around one atom
  • They form a shared pool or "sea" of electrons
  • Positive metal nuclei (or ions) are surrounded by this electron sea

Why Delocalization Matters

Delocalized electrons result in characteristic metal properties:

  • Electrical conductivity — electrons can move under an electric field
  • Thermal conductivity — electrons transfer heat efficiently
  • Malleability and ductility — atoms slide without breaking bonds
  • Luster — mobile electrons interact with light

Quick Comparison

Bond Type Typical Participants What Happens to Electrons Common Properties
Ionic Metal + Nonmetal Electrons transferred High MP, conducts when molten/aq
Covalent Nonmetal + Nonmetal Electrons shared Lower MP, no conductivity
Metallic Metal atoms Electrons delocalized Conductive as solid, malleable

Topic 2.2: Intramolecular Force and Potential Energy

This section explains how potential energy varies with the distance between atoms, how bond length and bond energy relate to covalent bond order and atomic size, and how Coulomb's law describes ionic interaction strength.

Potential Energy vs. Internuclear Distance

  • Internuclear distance is the distance between the nuclei
  • At large distances, atoms do not interact, so potential energy is near zero
  • As atoms get closer, attractive forces between the nuclei and electrons lower the potential energy
  • The lowest point on the curve is the equilibrium bond length
  • The depth of the curve below zero represents bond energy — the energy required to separate the atoms to infinite distance
  • If atoms get too close, repulsive forces dominate and potential energy rises sharply

Covalent Bond Length & Bond Order

Bond order refers to how many electron pairs are shared:

  • Single bond = 1 pair
  • Double bond = 2 pairs
  • Triple bond = 3 pairs
As bond order increases:
• Bond length decreases
• Bond energy increases — more energy is needed to break stronger bonds

This happens because more shared electrons create stronger attraction between the nuclei and the shared electrons.

Coulomb's Law & Ionic Interaction Strength

For ionic interactions (cations + anions), Coulomb's law describes how strongly the ions attract each other.

F ∝ (q₁ × q₂) / r²

Key implications for ionic bonds:

  • Larger charges (like 2+ & 2–) → stronger attraction
  • Smaller ions (smaller distance r) → stronger attraction

Coulomb's law explains why ions with larger charges and smaller radii form stronger ionic interactions — which shows up as deeper potential wells on a diagram.

A Quick Summary

Concept Key Relationship
Potential energy vs. internuclear distance Lowest PE = equilibrium bond length; depth = bond energy
Bond order (covalent) ↑ bond order → ↓ bond length, ↑ bond energy
Ionic interactions (Coulomb's law) Stronger when charges are larger and particles are closer

Topic 2.3: Structure of Ionic Solids

Ionic Solids Do Not Exist as Discrete Molecules

Unlike molecules like H₂O or CO₂, ionic solids are not made of individual ion pairs floating independently.

Instead, they form continuous crystal lattices — repeating 3‑D arrays of ions.

Particulate Model of an Ionic Solid

Particles Arranged in a Periodic 3‑D Array

An ionic solid can be represented by a particulate model with alternating cations and anions arranged in a regular pattern.

This arrangement ensures that:

  • Attractive forces (cation–anion) are maximized
  • Repulsive forces (like‑charge ion–ion) are minimized
  • This matches Coulomb's Law

Why This Arrangement is Stable

Maximizing Attraction

  • Opposite charges attract more strongly when the ions are near each other
  • Placing cations next to anions allows many strong attractions

Minimizing Repulsion

  • Like charges (cation–cation or anion–anion) are kept as far apart as possible
  • This reduces destabilizing repulsive forces throughout the lattice
Together, these effects lower the potential energy of the whole solid and create a highly stable structure.

What Coulomb's Law Predicts About Lattice Arrangement

  • More ion neighbors of opposite charge → stronger overall attraction
  • Charges matter: larger magnitude charges give stronger attraction
  • Distance matters: smaller ions allow closer packing and stronger attraction

This is why the crystal lattice forms with alternating charges — it produces the strongest net electrostatic attraction consistent with Coulomb's law.

Topic 2.4: Structure of Metals and Alloys

Metallic Bonding

Metallic bonding can be represented as:

  • An array of positive metal ions
  • Surrounded by a "sea of delocalized valence electrons"

Visualization of Metallic Bonding:

• Metal atoms contribute valence electrons to a delocalized electron cloud
• Positive metal ions are arranged in a 3‑D lattice
• Delocalized electrons flow throughout the structure

Why This Model Matters

  • Electrons are free to move → explains high electrical and thermal conductivity
  • Ions can slide past each other without bond breakage → explains malleability and ductility
  • Delocalized electrons reflect light → metallic luster
