UNIT 3

Civil Liberties and Civil Rights

Understanding Civil Liberties

Civil liberties are constitutional protections that prevent the government from interfering with individual freedoms. These rights ensure that the government doesn't overreach and that people remain protected from government abuse.

The Bill of Rights

The Bill of Rights consists of the Constitution's first ten amendments, intended to protect basic freedoms from government interference.

Key Distinction: Originally, the Bill of Rights only applied to the federal government, not state governments. This changed through the Incorporation Doctrine, which gradually applied these rights to the states through the Fourteenth Amendment.

First Amendment Freedoms

The Five Freedoms

The First Amendment protects five fundamental freedoms:

  • Freedom of Religion – Everyone can follow the religion of their choice
  • Freedom of Speech – Protection of verbal and written expression
  • Freedom of the Press – Media can operate without government censorship
  • Freedom of Assembly – People can gather peacefully
  • Freedom to Petition – Citizens can ask the government to address grievances

Religion Clauses

Establishment Clause

The government cannot support, favor, or establish an official religion. This maintains the separation of church and state, often called the wall-of-separation principle.

Engel v. Vitale – Banned school-led prayer in public schools, ruling that it violated the Establishment Clause.
Lemon v. Kurtzman – Created the Lemon Test for church-state cases. Government action must: (1) have a secular purpose, (2) not primarily advance or inhibit religion, and (3) not create excessive government entanglement with religion.
Abington v. Schempp – Schools cannot conduct sponsored Bible readings.
Zelman v. Simmons-Harris – School vouchers can be used at religious schools without violating the Establishment Clause.

Free Exercise Clause

Protects your right to practice your religion freely without government interference.

Conscientious Objector: An individual who refuses to fight in war due to moral or religious reasons, protected under free exercise principles.

Released Time: Students are allowed to leave school for religious instruction.

Freedom of Speech

Freedom of expression covers not just words, but also actions that communicate a message.

Limits on Speech

Clear and Present Danger Test: If speech creates an immediate, clear threat to public safety, it can be limited.
Schenck v. United States – Established that during wartime, there are limits on free speech when it poses a clear and present danger.
Gitlow v. New York – Started applying free speech protections to state governments through the Fourteenth Amendment.

Symbolic Speech

Symbolic speech refers to actions that represent a message, such as wearing armbands or burning a flag.

Texas v. Johnson – Flag burning is protected symbolic speech under the First Amendment.

Commercial Speech

Commercial speech includes advertising and content meant to sell products. It's protected, but not as strongly as political speech.

Obscenity

Roth v. United States – Obscene material is not protected by free speech.
Miller v. California – Set the three-part test for determining what counts as obscene material.

Freedom of the Press

Prior Restraint

Prior restraint occurs when the government tries to stop speech or publication before it happens. This is generally unconstitutional.

Near v. Minnesota – The government usually cannot censor something before it's published.

Libel and Public Figures

Libel refers to written false statements that harm someone's reputation.

Actual malice means intentionally lying or recklessly disregarding the truth. Public officials must prove actual malice in libel cases.

New York Times v. Sullivan – Public officials must prove actual malice to win libel cases, protecting robust debate about public figures.

Public figures are people in the spotlight who have less protection from criticism than private individuals.

Miami Herald v. Tornillo – The government cannot force newspapers to print opposing viewpoints.

Broadcast Media Regulation

Red Lion v. FCC – Allowed regulation of broadcast media because airwaves are a limited public resource.

Freedom of Association

NAACP v. Alabama – Protected freedom of association and the right to privacy in organizational membership.

Historical Context: Suppression of Speech

Sedition Act – Criminalized speaking or writing critically about the government.

Smith Act – Made it illegal to advocate overthrowing the government.

Communist Control Act – A Cold War law aimed at limiting communist political activity.

Internal Security Act – Required communist groups to register with the government.

Red Scare – A period when Americans were intensely afraid of communism due to its rapid global spread.

Preferred Position Doctrine

First Amendment rights receive preferred position – they get extra protection compared to other rights because they're fundamental to democracy.

Rights of the Accused

Fourth Amendment: Search and Seizure

Protects against unreasonable searches and seizures. You can't be searched without proper cause.

Probable cause is a reasonable belief that a crime has occurred, not just a hunch.

Search warrant is a court order that allows police to search a specific place.

Mapp v. Ohio – Established the exclusionary rule at the state level: evidence obtained illegally cannot be used in court.

Exclusionary rule means that if police find evidence illegally, they cannot use it in court.

Good-faith exception allows evidence to be used if police honestly believed they were following the law, even if technically they weren't.

Zurcher v. Stanford Daily – Police can search newsrooms if they have a valid warrant.

Fifth Amendment: Self-Incrimination and Due Process

The Fifth Amendment provides several protections:

  • Right to remain silent (protection against self-incrimination)
  • Due process – Government must follow fair procedures
  • No double jeopardy – Can't be tried twice for the same crime

Self-incrimination is the right not to testify against yourself and to remain silent.

Due Process Clause requires the government to follow fair rules before punishing someone.

Miranda v. Arizona – Police must inform suspects of their rights (right to remain silent, right to an attorney) before questioning.

Sixth Amendment: Right to Counsel and Fair Trial

The Sixth Amendment guarantees every criminal defendant:

  • A fair and speedy trial
  • An impartial jury
  • The right to an attorney
Gideon v. Wainwright – The state must provide a lawyer if you cannot afford one.

Plea bargaining occurs when defendants plead guilty in exchange for a lighter sentence, often because they believe they won't win at trial.

Eighth Amendment: Cruel and Unusual Punishment

The Eighth Amendment protects against cruel and unusual punishment. Punishments cannot be extreme or inhumane.

Gregg v. Georgia – The death penalty is constitutional, but only under specific, fair procedures.
McCleskey v. Kemp – The Court did not overturn the death penalty despite evidence of racial bias in sentencing.

The Right to Privacy

The right to privacy is not explicitly mentioned in the Constitution, but the Supreme Court has recognized it as implied by several amendments.

Roe v. Wade – Established a constitutional right to abortion based on the right to privacy. The Court ruled that states could not ban abortion in the first trimester.
Planned Parenthood v. Casey – Modified Roe by prohibiting states from banning abortion before fetal viability, but allowed some restrictions.

National Security and Privacy

FISA (Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act) allows secret courts to approve surveillance for national security reasons, balancing privacy with security concerns.

Key Constitutional Amendments

Fourteenth Amendment – Applies rights to the states through the Due Process Clause and guarantees equal protection under the law. This amendment is central to the incorporation doctrine.

Incorporation Doctrine – The process of applying the Bill of Rights protections to state governments through the Fourteenth Amendment.

Barron v. Baltimore – Early case establishing that the Bill of Rights originally did not apply to state governments, only to the federal government.

Balancing Rights and Government Interests

Least restrictive means – When the government restricts rights, it must use the least restrictive method to achieve its legitimate goal.

Balancing Test: Courts often balance individual liberties against government interests in public safety, national security, and order. The government must show a compelling reason to restrict fundamental rights.
